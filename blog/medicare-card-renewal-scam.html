The Medicare card renewal scam: 3 things you should never click

By Arian, founder · Senior Scam Shield May 12, 2026 6 min read
The short version

Medicare never texts or emails you out of the blue to "renew your card." If you get a message asking you to confirm your Social Security number, click a link, or pay a small fee — it is a scam, every single time. Real Medicare only contacts you by U.S. mail unless you've started a phone call yourself.

It almost always starts the same way. You get a text — or an email that looks like one of the cleaner ones in your inbox. There's a Medicare logo at the top. The wording is calm but urgent: "Your Medicare card is expiring. Renew now to avoid a lapse in coverage." There's a button. There's a deadline. There's no obvious typo.

This is the Medicare card renewal scam, and it works because it looks like something you'd expect to see. Most seniors I talk to don't get scammed by clearly broken messages — they get scammed by messages that pass the "looks fine to me" test.

So here's what to actually look at. Three things. If you only remember these, you'll catch this scam every time.

1. Your Medicare card doesn't expire

This is the single biggest tell. The red, white, and blue Medicare card you got when you enrolled does not have an expiration date and does not need to be renewed. Ever.

Medicare Advantage plans (the ones offered by private insurers like Humana or UnitedHealthcare) can change year to year — but those plan cards aren't your Medicare card. The federal Medicare card itself is permanent.

So the moment a message asks you to "renew" it, you can stop reading. There is no version of the truth where Medicare needs you to renew this card.

Red flag

Any phrase containing "renew your Medicare card," "card expiring," or "card replacement fee" is a scam. Real Medicare replacement cards are free at medicare.gov and you only request one if you've lost the original.

2. Real Medicare doesn't ask for your Social Security number

The reason these scams exist is to harvest your Social Security number. Every other detail in the message — the urgency, the "small processing fee," the official-looking form — is just packaging to get you to type those nine digits into a form.

If you've been on Medicare since 2018, your Medicare number is not your Social Security number anymore. It's an 11-character "Medicare Beneficiary Identifier" (MBI). Medicare already knows it. They have no reason to ask you to confirm it, and certainly no reason to ask for your SSN.

Any form that asks you to "verify" your SSN to keep your benefits active is collecting it for identity theft. That's the whole product. The fake site exists for that one form field.

3. Medicare contacts you by U.S. mail

This is the rule that catches almost everything. If you didn't start the conversation, the only legitimate way Medicare communicates with you is by physical mail. Not text. Not email. Not phone (unless you called them first and they're calling you back).

That doesn't mean every Medicare-themed email is a scam — your insurance plan may email you about benefits, claims, or annual enrollment. But unsolicited messages from "Medicare" itself with a link to "verify" or "renew" are scams, full stop.

Safe move

If you're unsure whether something is real, ignore the link in the message and go directly to medicare.gov or call 1-800-MEDICARE (1-800-633-4227). Never use a phone number or link the message gave you — that's the entire trick.

What to do if you already clicked

First, don't panic. Clicking a link by itself rarely does damage. The danger is in what you typed into the page after.

  • If you only clicked but didn't enter anything: close the page and you're almost certainly fine. Run a quick antivirus scan if you want extra reassurance.
  • If you entered your Social Security number: place a free fraud alert with one of the credit bureaus — Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion (calling one of them alerts the other two). Consider freezing your credit. Report the incident at reportfraud.ftc.gov.
  • If you entered a card number or paid a "fee": call the bank or card issuer immediately — that line on the back of the card. They can reverse the charge and reissue the card before more damage happens.

The pattern, in one sentence

Almost every senior-targeted scam follows the same shape: create urgency, mention an official agency, ask you to act outside the agency's actual channels. Medicare's renewal scam, the Social Security suspension robocall, fake IRS letters — they all share that skeleton. Once you see the shape, you stop falling for the costume.
