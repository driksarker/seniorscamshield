The grandparent scam, in their own words — what to listen for

By Drik, co-founder · Senior Scam Shield May 19, 2026 7 min read
The short version

The grandparent scam is a phone call where someone pretends to be your grandchild in trouble — usually arrested, in an accident, or stranded — and begs you not to tell their parents. They want cash, gift cards, or wire transfers, sent fast. The fix is a single question: "What's the name of your dog?" Or anything only the real grandchild would know. One verification question ends the call instantly.

I want to tell you about a call my own grandmother got in 2024. I have permission to share it. The reason I'm sharing it is because it matches, almost word for word, every grandparent scam call we hear about — and once you've heard the pattern, you'll never miss it.

The phone rings around 4 p.m. A young man's voice. Sniffling.

"Grandma? It's me. I'm in trouble. Please don't tell Mom and Dad."

Three sentences. The entire scam architecture is already in place. Let me show you what's happening in each.

"Grandma?" — fishing for a name

The caller doesn't say which grandchild they are. They wait for you to fill in the blank. The moment you say "Tommy, is that you?" — they're now Tommy for the rest of the call. They picked the name; you handed it to them.

This is the most reliable tell of all. Your real grandchild knows their own name. They would say it.

"I'm in trouble" — flooding the channel with urgency

Once you've named the grandchild, the next 30 seconds are about getting your heart rate up. Common variations:

  • "I was in a car accident. There was a kid in the other car…"
  • "I got arrested. I blew over the limit at a roadblock."
  • "I'm stranded in Mexico, my passport got stolen…"
  • "I broke my nose in the accident, that's why I sound different."

That last one — "that's why I sound different" — is genius and horrible. It explains the voice mismatch before you notice it. Real grandchildren don't pre-explain their voice. Scammers do.

"Don't tell Mom and Dad" — cutting off your verification

This is the line that should stop the call cold. Every grandparent scam includes some version of it, because the entire scam depends on you not calling anyone else to check. The "shame" framing — "please, I'm so embarrassed" — is engineered to keep you isolated for the next 15 minutes.

Red flag

If someone calls claiming to be a family member in trouble and asks you not to tell other family members — that is the scam. Real emergencies don't come with secrecy requirements. Real lawyers, real police, real hospitals do not ask grandparents to keep things from parents.

The hand-off

After the grandchild "softens you up," the call often gets passed to an "officer" or "lawyer" or "bail bondsman." Now the tone shifts from panicked to authoritative.

"Ma'am, your grandson is in our custody. Bail has been set at $4,800. We need it in cash or gift cards, immediately, or he stays here overnight."

This second voice does the actual extraction. The grandchild voice was for emotion; the officer voice is for instructions.

The four impossibilities

Here is what a real arrest looks like:

  1. Bail is paid at the courthouse or via a licensed bail bondsman. Not in cash dropped at a gas station. Not in Apple gift cards. Not by wire to "the clerk."
  2. Police don't accept payment over the phone. Ever. From anyone.
  3. Lawyers don't take retainer fees in iTunes cards. If anyone tells you they do, they aren't a lawyer.
  4. Real emergencies survive a verification phone call. If your grandson is genuinely in jail, he'll still be in jail in five minutes when you call his mom.

The one question that ends the call

Safe move

Ask one question that only the real grandchild would know. The dog's name. Your nickname for them. Where you took them on their last birthday. Pick something a stranger couldn't guess from Facebook. "Hey honey — what was the name of our old dog?" The scammer will hang up. Real grandchildren answer in half a second.

I'll give you a slightly stronger version. Don't use information the scammer could find online — the grandchild's school, their car, their hometown. Use something between the two of you. A private joke. A nickname only you use. A pet that died ten years ago and was never on social media.

The reason this works is simple: scammers are running dozens of calls at once. They don't have the bandwidth to research your specific family. The instant they hit a real-life detail check, they move on to the next number.

What to do if you already sent money

Time matters more than embarrassment. Within the first few hours, money is sometimes recoverable. After 24 hours, almost never. So:

  • Wire transfer: call the company you wired through (Western Union, MoneyGram, your bank) and ask them to halt or recall the transfer. The faster, the better.
  • Gift cards: call the card brand's fraud line — the number on the back of the card. Some can freeze the balance if you act within minutes.
  • Cash sent by mail or courier: contact the carrier (FedEx, UPS, USPS) immediately and request the package be held.
  • Then report it: file with the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov, your local police (a paper trail helps), and the FBI's IC3 at ic3.gov.

And tell your family. Not because you owe anyone the embarrassment — because the scammers often try again, sometimes within days, on the same household.

A small culture shift

Here's the thing about elder fraud that doesn't get talked about enough: most of the calls don't succeed. They succeed because the victim was alone with the decision. The single biggest defense isn't a piece of technology — it's having someone you can call before you act. Not someone who'll judge. Someone who'll say, "Let me hear it. Let's look at it together."
