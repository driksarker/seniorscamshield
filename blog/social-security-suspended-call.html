"Your Social Security has been suspended." Is it real? (No.)

By Arian, founder · Senior Scam Shield May 15, 2026 5 min read
The short version

A Social Security number cannot be "suspended." The Social Security Administration does not arrest people, demand payment by gift card, or call you out of the blue with threats. If a recorded voice says any of those things, it's a scam — hang up, don't press any buttons, and don't call any number the message gave you.

If you've gotten this call, you know how unnerving it sounds. A flat, robotic voice. A serious tone. Something about your Social Security number being "linked to suspicious activity in Texas," and a warrant for your arrest unless you press 1 right now.

This is the most-reported phone scam in America. The Federal Trade Commission gets hundreds of thousands of reports about it every year, and the dollar amounts seniors lose to it are staggering — often their entire savings, moved out of their accounts in a single afternoon.

Here's exactly what it sounds like, and exactly why every line of it is impossible.

The call, line by line

"This is the Social Security Administration. Your Social Security number has been suspended due to suspicious activity. Please press 1 to speak with an officer, or you will be arrested within 24 hours."

Four lies in four sentences. Let's go through them.

Lie #1: "This is the Social Security Administration"

The SSA almost never calls. They communicate by mail. If they do call — and it's rare — it's only after you've already been in contact with them about something specific (like an application or appeal you started). They will never robocall you out of nowhere.

Lie #2: "Your Social Security number has been suspended"

This isn't a thing. A Social Security number is not an account. It cannot be turned off, suspended, locked, frozen, or revoked. The number you were assigned when you were born or naturalized stays with you for life. The fact that the scammers chose a phrase that sounds plausible but isn't real is the entire mechanism of the scam — it makes you panic before you can think.

Lie #3: "Press 1 to speak with an officer"

Real federal agencies do not give you menu options to "press 1 to avoid arrest." This is a casino-style call funnel — they're sorting victims from non-victims. People who press 1 get routed to a live "agent" (a scammer) who escalates the urgency until you're being walked to a Walgreens to buy gift cards.

Lie #4: "Or you will be arrested within 24 hours"

The SSA does not have arrest authority. Even agencies that do (like the FBI or the IRS) don't call you ahead of time to warn you about it. The "24-hour arrest threat" is the giveaway. No real official ever uses it.

Red flag

If the caller asks you to pay anything in gift cards, wire transfer, cryptocurrency, or by reading numbers off the back of a "safety deposit" voucher — it is 100% a scam. No exceptions. No government agency in the United States accepts gift cards as payment.

Why this scam works on smart people

People sometimes blame themselves after falling for this call. "I should have known." But these calls are engineered to bypass the part of your brain that catches scams. Three things make that happen:

  1. Authority. The voice claims to be from a federal agency. Most of us are trained to take that seriously.
  2. Urgency. "24 hours" or "right now" doesn't give you time to call someone else and check.
  3. Fear of consequences. The threat is your benefits, your money, your liberty. The bigger the threat, the harder it is to think clearly.

Combine those and even cautious people freeze. That's the point. The scammers aren't trying to convince you it's real — they're trying to convince you it might be real, just for the 60 seconds it takes to get your information.

What to do when you get the call

Safe move

Hang up. Don't press any number — not even 2 to "be removed from the list." Pressing anything tells the system you're a live person and your phone number gets sold to other scam operations.

If you want to verify whether anything is actually going on with your Social Security:

  • Call the SSA directly at 1-800-772-1213 (their real public number). Don't use any number the robocall mentioned.
  • Or log in to your account at ssa.gov/myaccount.
  • Report the scam call at oig.ssa.gov (SSA's Office of the Inspector General) and at reportfraud.ftc.gov.

If you already pressed 1, talked to a "supervisor," or sent any money — stop the bleeding immediately. Call your bank. Call your card issuer. Freeze your credit with all three bureaus. And tell someone you trust, today. Speed matters more than embarrassment.

One last thing

If you have a parent or grandparent who is going to hear this call eventually, the best thing you can do isn't to lecture them about it — it's to give them a person to call before they make any decision. A real person who'll tell them it's fake in 30 seconds and they can move on with their day.
